The ARTHA Framework™
A proprietary AI adoption methodology purpose-built for regulated industries. Five dimensions, scored, benchmarked, and continuously tracked.

In Sanskrit, Artha means purpose, meaning, and value — one of the four fundamental goals of human pursuit. It is the recognition that prosperity is not accidental. It is built through deliberate, structured action.

That is exactly what regulated industry organizations need when they approach AI: not another generic "AI transformation" framework, but a deliberate, structured methodology that reveals exactly where they stand, what is blocking them, and the precise sequence of actions to move forward — compliantly, confidently, and with measurable results.

88% use AI. Only 6% capture meaningful value.
In regulated industries, the gap is wider and the consequences of failure are higher: regulatory penalties, patient harm, financial losses, reputational damage.

The failure is never the AI itself. It is always one or more of five structural gaps — and ARTHA addresses all five, sequentially, measurably, and in the right order.

How ARTHA Is Applied

1. ARTHA Diagnostic Assessment

A structured evaluation across all five dimensions. Combines quantitative scoring (weighted questions across governance, data, people, regulatory, and measurement), qualitative interviews (C-suite, department heads, frontline), document review (existing policies, governance docs, data architecture), and a technology audit (systems inventory, AI tools in use, shadow AI).

Output: ARTHA Score + Dimension Heatmap + Priority Gap Map.

2. ARTHA Gap Analysis

For each dimension scoring below the Structured tier, a structured root cause analysis identifies what is specifically missing, what the downstream consequences are, and what must be resolved before the next dimension can advance.

3. ARTHA Roadmap

A sequenced 36-month implementation plan, tied to business outcomes (not technology milestones), regulatory compliance deadlines, ROI capture targets, and workforce transformation checkpoints.

4. ARTHA Quarterly Scorecard

Ongoing quarterly re-measurement across all five dimensions with progress tracking against baseline, industry benchmark comparison, AI value realization tracking (EBIT impact), and regulatory compliance status.

Why Order Matters

Most AI consulting starts with technology. ARTHA starts with people and regulation — because those are the two most common failure points in regulated industries, and the two that take the longest to fix.

You cannot govern what your people don't understand. You cannot build on infrastructure that violates your regulations. You cannot measure what you haven't governed.

What Each Dimension Actually Measures

Each dimension is assessed across 12 structured questions scored 0–3. Below is the full sub-dimension breakdown — what ARTHA is specifically looking for inside each area.

A
Adoption — People, Culture & AI Readiness
The dimension most organizations underestimate and most consulting firms skip entirely.
Leadership AI Literacy
Can the C-suite articulate what AI can and cannot do in their specific regulatory context? Does the board ask the right questions?
Workforce Change Readiness
Structured change management plans in place. Employee communication cadence. Fear vs. curiosity ratio in workforce sentiment.
AI Skills Inventory
Current skills mapped against required capability. Training roadmap exists and is funded. Shadow AI usage tracked.
Cultural Experimentation
Does the organization run structured AI pilots? Is failure treated as learning? Are cross-functional AI teams empowered?
Score 0–25 looks like:Leadership references "AI strategy" in presentations but has no working knowledge. Employees fear job displacement. No formal training. Multiple unsanctioned AI tools in use.
Score 76–100 looks like:Every department head can name 3 AI use cases in their domain. Active communities of practice. Structured reskilling with career pathways. Shadow AI inventory and governance.
R
Regulatory — Compliance Architecture
The dimension that creates the highest-consequence failures. Non-compliance in regulated industries is not a cost — it is an existential event.
Regulatory Landscape Mapping
US federal + state + EU AI Act obligations mapped to specific AI use cases. Obligations prioritized by deadline and penalty severity.
AI-Specific Compliance Controls
Model documentation, bias testing, explainability requirements, audit trails, and human-in-the-loop checkpoints for high-stakes decisions.
Data Privacy & Consent Architecture
Training data provenance. Patient/customer consent for AI use. Cross-border data transfer compliance for AI model APIs.
Third-Party AI Vendor Governance
Vendor contracts establish liability and compliance responsibility. AI vendor risk assessments. SLAs cover compliance incidents.
Score 0–25 looks like:Legal has reviewed one vendor contract. No AI-specific compliance framework. No one knows which HIPAA/FCRA/NYDFS rules apply to the AI tools already deployed. EU AI Act not mapped.
Score 76–100 looks like:Regulatory change management process active. Each AI use case has a documented compliance assessment. Audit-ready AI model registry. Legal + compliance embedded in AI project teams.
T
Technology — Infrastructure & Data Foundation
AI is only as strong as the data it runs on. Most organizations discover their data problems during AI deployment — not before it.
Data Quality & Lineage
Data quality scores exist and are tracked. Data lineage is documented. Golden records defined for high-stakes AI inputs (patient, customer, transaction).
AI Infrastructure Readiness
Compute capacity for model inference. MLOps tooling in place. Model versioning, monitoring, and rollback capability.
Integration Architecture
AI outputs can reach the systems that need them (EHR, core banking, claims). APIs are documented, stable, and auditable.
Security Architecture for AI
Prompt injection controls. Model output filtering. Data access controls specific to AI pipelines. Adversarial attack surface assessed.
Score 0–25 looks like:Data in silos with no cross-system identifiers. No data quality metrics. Cloud infrastructure exists but is not AI-workload-ready. No ML monitoring in place.
Score 76–100 looks like:Unified data platform with documented quality SLAs. AI-ready integration layer. Model cards for every deployed model. Automated drift detection with alert routing to compliance.
H
Holistic — Strategy & Governance
AI governance is not a policy document. It is an operating system — the rules by which every AI decision in the organization gets made, reviewed, and corrected.
AI Strategy & Portfolio
Board-approved AI strategy. Use case portfolio prioritized by impact vs. compliance risk. Resource allocation aligned to strategy.
AI Ethics & Risk Tiering
AI use cases classified by risk tier. Ethics review process for Tier 1 (high-risk) uses. Prohibited uses defined in policy.
Human-in-the-Loop Design
All high-stakes AI outputs have defined human override mechanisms. Escalation procedures exist and are tested. Override logging.
Cross-Functional Governance Structure
AI governance committee with defined charter, meeting cadence, and decision authority. IT, legal, compliance, and business all represented.
Score 0–25 looks like:AI decisions made in IT or by individual department heads. No governance committee. No risk classification. Ethics is a PR statement, not an operating process.
Score 76–100 looks like:Functioning AI governance board with escalation authority. Published AI principles that are operationalized. Quarterly risk register review. Every Tier 1 use case has a human appeal mechanism.
A
Accountability — Measurement & Value Realization
If you cannot measure AI's contribution to EBIT, you cannot defend the investment — and you cannot scale what you cannot justify.
ROI Methodology
Defined metrics for each AI use case. Baseline measurements taken before deployment. Attribution methodology separates AI contribution from other factors.
Value Realization Tracking
Executive dashboards showing AI ROI. Business case owners accountable for outcomes. Underperforming AI investments reviewed and reallocated.
Scaling Gates
Clear criteria for when an AI pilot moves to enterprise deployment. Scaling decisions based on measured outcomes, not internal enthusiasm.
Audit & Reporting
AI performance reports generated for board, regulators, and business owners. Negative outcomes documented and learned from. External audit capability.
Score 0–25 looks like:ROI tracked by headcount savings only. No pre-deployment baselines. "AI is working great" is based on anecdote. No one knows the actual cost per AI outcome.
Score 76–100 looks like:EBIT-attributed AI value tracked per use case. Scaling gates enforced. Underperforming use cases retired. Annual external AI performance audit.

Critical Red Flags

ARTHA identifies four patterns that, when present, indicate an organization is at high risk of a compliance incident, AI initiative failure, or both — regardless of overall score. These are elevated immediately to leadership.

RF-001 — Adoption–Regulatory Inversion
Technology deployed before people or compliance are ready
Technology score ≥ 60 while Adoption score < 40 AND Regulatory score < 50. Common in organizations that let IT drive AI strategy without HR or legal at the table.
Impact: Low adoption rates, compliance incidents, forced rollbacks. Average cost of remediation: 2–4× original implementation cost.
RF-002 — Governance Vacuum
AI in production with no governance structure
Holistic score < 35 with any other dimension ≥ 55. AI tools are live and producing outputs that affect customers, patients, or financial decisions — but no one owns the outcomes.
Impact: When an AI decision causes harm, there is no accountability structure. Regulatory exposure is maximum. Personal liability risk for executives.
RF-003 — Shadow AI Proliferation
Untracked AI tool use across the organization
Adoption score < 40 with Technology score between 30–60. Multiple departments are using consumer AI tools (ChatGPT, Copilot, etc.) for work involving regulated data — without disclosure, policy, or controls.
Impact: Immediate data privacy exposure. Patient/customer PII leaving the regulatory perimeter. Undiscoverable in an audit.
RF-004 — Value Measurement Blind Spot
No attribution methodology for AI ROI
Accountability score < 30 with any other dimension ≥ 55. Significant AI investment is live but there is no methodology to attribute financial outcomes — making next-year budget defense impossible and scaling decisions arbitrary.
Impact: AI investments will be cut in the next budget cycle regardless of actual performance. Scaling decisions are made on enthusiasm, not evidence.

ARTHA in Regulated Industries

The framework is calibrated for sectors where AI failures carry institutional, regulatory, or patient-harm consequences — not just competitive disadvantage.

🏥
Healthcare & Life Sciences
HIPAA · ONC 21st Century Cures · FDA AI/ML · EU AI Act Article 22
AI in clinical decision support, prior authorization, and revenue cycle creates direct patient harm and regulatory exposure when deployed without a complete compliance architecture. The FDA's evolving framework for AI-based Software as a Medical Device (SaMD) is largely unaddressed in most healthcare AI programs.
Hardest dimension: Regulatory — FDA, OCR, and state AG oversight simultaneously.
🏦
Financial Services & Banking
ECOA · Fair Housing Act · FCRA · SR 11-7 (OCC) · NYDFS Part 500
Model risk management (SR 11-7) requires documentation, validation, and ongoing monitoring for every model used in credit, pricing, and fraud detection. AI amplifies both the scale of benefit and the scale of discriminatory harm. Explainability for adverse action notices is non-negotiable and technically demanding.
Hardest dimension: Holistic — governance must satisfy multiple regulators simultaneously.
🛡️
Insurance
State insurance regulations · NAIC Model Bulletin · Colorado SB 21-169
AI in underwriting, rating, and claims carries state-specific regulatory exposure — each state has different rules for algorithm transparency and anti-discrimination. Rate filing requirements limit how quickly AI-driven pricing changes can be deployed. Customer fairness testing must be ongoing, not point-in-time.
Hardest dimension: Regulatory — 50-state insurance regulation mosaic.
🔬
Pharmaceutical & Biotech
FDA 21 CFR Part 11 · GxP · ICH E6 (GCP) · EU AI Act High-Risk
AI in clinical trials, drug discovery, and manufacturing quality control must satisfy Good Practice (GxP) validation requirements — which predate AI and were not designed for probabilistic systems. Computer System Validation (CSV) frameworks require extensive documentation, often at odds with iterative AI development cycles.
Hardest dimension: Regulatory — FDA validation rigor applied to non-deterministic systems.

What an ARTHA Engagement Looks Like

A full diagnostic is a structured, time-boxed engagement — not an open-ended consulting retainer. Here is the standard process from kickoff to scorecard.

Week 1 — Days 1–3
Kickoff & Pre-Work Package
Sponsor briefing with C-suite. Document collection request issued (governance policies, data architecture docs, existing AI inventory, regulatory assessments). Assessment scheduling for department heads and key stakeholders across all five dimensions.
Deliverable: Pre-work checklist + assessment schedule
Week 1–2 — Days 3–10
Structured Assessment & Interviews
60-question assessment completed by relevant roles for each dimension (not one person filling out the entire form — the right respondent per question). Qualitative interviews with department heads. Shadow AI audit via IT helpdesk data and browser plugin inventories where accessible.
Deliverable: Raw scoring data + interview notes
Week 2 — Days 10–12
Analysis & Red Flag Identification
Scoring engine calculates dimension scores and composite ARTHA Score. Industry weights applied. Red flag screening (RF-001 through RF-004). Industry benchmark comparison against peer group. Draft gap analysis produced per below-threshold dimension.
Deliverable: Internal analysis document
Week 3 — Days 14–16
Findings Presentation
Executive readout: ARTHA Score + radar chart, dimension breakdown, red flags, industry benchmark comparison. Department-level findings by dimension. Priority gap map with impact vs. effort quadrant. Initial roadmap recommendations framed by budget cycle and regulatory deadlines.
Deliverable: Executive presentation + 30-page diagnostic report
Week 4 — Days 18–21
ARTHA Roadmap Design
36-month implementation roadmap sequenced in ARTHA order. Each initiative tied to a business outcome, regulatory deadline, or risk reduction target. Resource requirements estimated. Quick wins (0–90 days) identified for early momentum. Scoring retargets set for Q1 and Q4.
Deliverable: ARTHA Roadmap + 90-day quick-win plan
Quarterly — Every 90 Days
ARTHA Scorecard & Benchmark Update
Re-measurement against baseline on key indicators per dimension. Progress tracking against roadmap milestones. Industry benchmark updated as peer group data refreshes. Executive scorecard issued. Roadmap re-sequenced if red flags resolved or new regulatory requirements surface.
Deliverable: Quarterly ARTHA Scorecard
